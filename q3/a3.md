A race car infotainment system is fundamentally different from a normal mobile app because the consequences of failure are physical. 
A mobile app can tolerate a laggy render or a dropped network call, but at 200 km/h, stale brake temperature data or a missed fault alert can end a race or injure a driver. 
Every design decision must account for extreme vibration, 40°C+ ambient temperatures, unreliable CANbus telemetry, and a driver 
whose mind is almost entirely consumed by racing. There are no menus, no scrolling, no second chances to read something. 
Information must be processed in under a second at a glance.

Four principles guide good infotainment design:
Prioritisation of critical information: the display must establish a clear visual hierarchy. Speed, power state, and active faults dominate; 
historical or low-urgency data sits at the periphery. If a motor over-temperature warning competes visually with lap delta, the driver may process the wrong one first.
Fail-safe behaviour: when a sensor feed drops or a CAN frame is corrupted, the system must never silently display stale data as current. 
Fields should grey out or show a "last known" timestamp. A frozen tyre pressure reading that looks live could cause a driver to ignore a real blowout risk.
Predictability and UI consistency: the driver builds a mental map of the screen over hours of practice. If layout shifts under fault conditions, 
or warning colours are inconsistent between sessions, that map breaks down precisely when it matters most. Consistency is a safety property, not just a design preference.
Robustness to corrupted or partial data: CANbus frames can arrive out of order, truncated, or with checksum errors. The system must detect and discard these 
rather than render nonsense values. A tyre pressure display showing 312 PSI because of an unvalidated corrupt frame is worse than showing nothing.

For testing, the system needs to be validated against the actual operating environment, not just clean lab conditions. 
This means replaying recorded telemetry logs at 2-5x speed to stress the rendering pipeline and expose memory leaks or UI thread starvation. 
Fault injection (deliberately dropping packets, corrupting frames, simulating CAN bus silence) verifies that graceful degradation actually works. 
Hardware should be tested in a heat chamber at 50°C+ and on a vibration rig to catch display flicker, touch drift, and GPU throttling. 
Driver readability sessions, where test subjects must identify specific values under time pressure, measure real-world comprehension speed. 
Finally, long-duration soak tests running for the full expected race session catch cumulative issues like memory growth that only appear over time.

The goal throughout is a system the driver can trust completely, one that tells them exactly what they need, hides nothing important, and fails loudly rather than silently.
